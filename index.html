📐 Scientific Model: Why do seasons happen?

✅ Earth orbits the Sun with its axis tilted 23.5°, always pointing toward Polaris (North Star). When the Northern Hemisphere tilts toward the Sun, it receives more direct sunlight → longer days → Summer. When it tilts away, sunlight is less direct → shorter days → Winter. The Southern Hemisphere experiences opposite seasons.
This model clearly shows fixed axial tilt (visualized by the white line) and how orbital position determines seasons. Drag the Earth to see spring, summer, autumn, and winter labels change context — perfect for young learners.

🌏 Science Behind the Seasons

Earth revolves around the Sun in an elliptical orbit. Because of the 23.5° axial tilt, the subsolar point moves between the Tropic of Cancer and the Tropic of Capricorn over a year. At the vernal equinox, day and night are equal worldwide. At the summer solstice, the Northern Hemisphere receives maximum sunlight. The model demonstrates how the position along the orbit determines which hemisphere is tilted toward the Sun. Without axial tilt, there would be no seasons (NASA, 2024).

📖 Why do scientists use models?

Models make enormous, slow, or invisible systems understandable. They simplify complex ideas into hands-on experiences. A dragable Earth orbiting a model Sun makes the abstract concept of "tilt + orbit" tangible and memorable (National Geographic Education, 2023; UCAR, 2022).
